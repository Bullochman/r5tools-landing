# Voiceover tips — recording r5tools.io TikToks on your phone

Written for Evan. Everything in this doc is designed for a one-person shop with a phone and 15 minutes.

## Gear (what you already have is enough)

- **iPhone mic is fine.** Do NOT buy a Blue Yeti or a lav mic for this. LWS players are watching on phone speakers in a noisy room — production polish is invisible to them and often works against you (over-produced audio reads as "ad"). Real voice reads as "real R5."
- **Record in a small carpeted room** or a closet with clothes hanging. This kills reverb better than any $200 mic.
- **Hold the phone about a fist's distance from your mouth** — closer than that, plosives blow out; further, reverb creeps in.
- **AirPods Pro mic is acceptable** as a fallback if the room echoes. Do not use regular AirPods — mic quality is noticeably worse.

## App

- **CapCut** — free, iOS + Android + desktop. Handles video, voiceover overlay, captions, B-roll, trending audio library. Everything below assumes CapCut but any editor works the same way.
- Alternative: TikTok's native editor is workable but weaker on caption timing.

## Recording flow (do this every time)

1. **Read the script out loud twice before recording.** Once for pacing, once for stumbles. Do not read cold.
2. **Record video and audio separately.** Film the B-roll (screen recording of the tool, gameplay clip, etc.) first, silent. Record voiceover as a separate audio pass over the finished video. This makes editing 10× easier — you can trim beats without disturbing the voice.
3. **Do 3 voiceover takes minimum.** Take 1 is always stiff. Take 2 is usually best. Take 3 is your backup if take 2 has a weird plosive.
4. **Listen back on your phone speaker, not headphones.** Headphones lie about how it will sound in the wild.

## The first 3 seconds — the only ones that matter

If the first 3 seconds don't earn the watch, nothing after them matters.

- **Say something specific in the first sentence.** "I lost my base at 3 AM" beats "Hey guys today we're going to talk about." A number, a mistake, a specific claim.
- **The visual must move.** A static screen for 3 seconds = scroll. Even if it's just a slow zoom on a screenshot, motion holds attention.
- **The first on-screen text overlay should be legible in one glance.** 4-6 words max, big font. Muted viewers only see the text — if it's a paragraph, they scroll.
- **Never open with a logo.** Save r5tools.io branding for the CTA. Opening with a logo signals "ad" and viewers bounce.

## Energy — keep it up without shouting

- **Match your speaking energy to an R5 hyping the alliance in Discord voice chat.** Not a news anchor. Not a hype-beast. Somewhere in between — "I've got something you need to hear."
- **Stand up while recording.** Voice carries more energy standing than sitting. This is not a myth.
- **Smile while you speak.** Even a small one. It changes the timbre of the voice in a way viewers subconsciously read as "friendly."
- **Cut your own silences.** Any pause longer than half a second in a 30-second video is dead space. Trim it in CapCut.

## Don't over-produce

Some pressure to look like a "professional" YouTuber will creep in. Resist it.

- **No cinematic B-roll.** Screen recordings of the actual tool, actual gameplay, actual member lists. Viewers can tell the difference between real R5 content and a marketing agency's stock footage.
- **No music bed unless the trending sound requires it.** Music competes with the voiceover on phone speakers. Silent B-roll with a clean voiceover beats music + voiceover every time on TikTok.
- **No fancy transitions.** Hard cuts only. Wipes, spins, and effects read as amateur when done in a 30-second gaming video.
- **Do not use AI voices.** Viewers spot them, and gaming audiences especially resent them. Your voice, always.

## Captions (on-screen text)

- **CapCut has an auto-caption feature.** Turn it on. Then hand-fix errors (it will screw up "R5" and game jargon every time).
- **Position captions at the top third of the screen**, not the bottom — TikTok's own UI eats the bottom third with icons and text.
- **Use a bold sans-serif font.** No script fonts. No thin fonts. If it's not legible at arm's length on a phone, it's not legible.
- **Match caption color to importance.** White for normal, yellow for the punchline, red for a warning ("Your base freezes here").

## Common failure modes

- **Recording in a hurry.** If you don't have 15 minutes, don't post. A rushed video underperforms and drags the account's average watch-time down for weeks.
- **Reading the script instead of talking through it.** Internalize the beats, then speak. Reading always sounds like reading.
- **Filming the phone screen with another phone.** Use the native screen recorder (iOS: Control Center → Record). Filming a screen produces moire patterns and looks unprofessional.
- **Forgetting the CTA.** Every video ends with "r5tools.io, code RONY-FREE." Every single one. If it's not there, the video did marketing for nobody.

## Batch shooting

Best process, tested for solo creators:

1. Sunday afternoon: read all 4 scripts for the week aloud, do minor rewrites for your natural speaking rhythm.
2. Sunday evening: record all 4 voiceovers back-to-back. 20 minutes.
3. Monday morning: film all 4 B-roll passes (screen recordings, game footage). 30 minutes.
4. Monday evening: edit all 4 videos in CapCut. 45 minutes.
5. Schedule all 4 in TikTok's native scheduler (or CapCut's) with the right captions and hashtags. Done for the week.

Total: about 2 hours a week for 4 posts. Sustainable.
